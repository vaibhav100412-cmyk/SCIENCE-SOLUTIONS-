📍 Ch 1 — Resources & Development

Introduces the concept of resources, their classification, sustainable development, and India's resource planning challenges.

🔑 What is a Resource?

  • Anything that satisfies a human need and has utility is a resource. Technology + knowledge converts materials into resources. Example: Coal became a resource only when humans learned to burn it for energy.

📂 Classification of Resources

  • Biotic: From living organisms — forests, animals, fisheries, crops, livestock.
  • Abiotic: Non-living — rocks, metals, minerals, water, air, soil.
  • Renewable: Replenished naturally — solar, wind, water, forests (if managed well).
  • Non-Renewable: Take millions of years to form, cannot be replaced — coal, petroleum, natural gas, minerals.
  • Natural Resources: Provided by nature — land, water, forests, minerals.
  • Human-Made Resources: Created by humans — buildings, roads, machines, technology.
  • Human Resources: People with knowledge and skills — the MOST important resource of all.

📊 Stock → Reserves → Actual Resources

  • Stock: Materials available but technology not yet developed to use them. Example: Hydrogen in water as fuel.
  • Reserves: Part of stock usable with existing technology but not yet started. Example: River water for hydropower in remote regions.
  • Actual Resources: Surveyed, quantity known, currently being used. Example: Coal mined in Jharkhand.
  • Potential Resources: Known to exist but not utilised due to lack of infrastructure or technology.

♻️ Sustainable Development

  • Meets present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet their needs.
  • Resource Planning Steps: (1) Identification of resources across regions, (2) Creating a planning structure, (3) Matching resources with national development plans.

⚠️ Problems from Misuse

  • Depletion for future | Environmental degradation | Inequality | Global warming | Deforestation | Loss of biodiversity.

🌾 Ch 2 — Agriculture

Agriculture is India's backbone — covering crop seasons, farming types, major crops, the Green Revolution and India's agricultural achievements.

🗓️ Three Crop Seasons

  • Kharif (June–Oct): Sown with monsoon (June-July), harvested Sept-Oct. Examples: Paddy, Maize, Cotton, Jute, Groundnut, Soyabean. Need lots of water and warmth.
  • Rabi (Oct–Apr): Sown October-December in winter, harvested March-April. Examples: Wheat, Gram, Peas, Mustard, Linseed. Need cool temperatures.
  • Zaid (Mar–Jun): Summer crops. Examples: Watermelon, Cucumber, Muskmelon, Bitter gourd.

🚜 Types of Farming

  • Subsistence: For family consumption. Small land, traditional tools, family labour, low output.
  • Intensive Subsistence: Dense population areas, very hard labour on small plots. High yield per unit area.
  • Commercial: Large scale for market/profit. Modern technology, HYV seeds, fertilisers. Example: Punjab/Haryana wheat farms.
  • Plantation: Single crop on large estate. Tea (Assam), Coffee (Karnataka), Rubber (Kerala), Sugar (UP).
  • Mixed Farming: Crops and livestock together. Animals give manure, crops give animal feed.
  • Shifting Cultivation (Jhum): Clear forest → farm → abandon to recover. Tribal practice. Also called slash-and-burn.
  • Horticulture: Fruits, vegetables, flowers. India = one of world's largest producers.
  • Organic Farming: Natural manures and bio-pesticides only. Environmentally sustainable.

🌾 Major Crops

  • Rice: Largest producer = West Bengal. Also Assam, Odisha, AP. Needs 100-200 cm rainfall.
  • Wheat: Punjab, Haryana, Western UP. Needs cool winter + 50-75 cm rainfall.
  • Cotton ("White Gold"): Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP, AP. Kharif crop.
  • Jute ("Golden Fibre"): West Bengal, Bihar, Assam. For bags, ropes, gunny sacks.
  • Sugarcane: UP (largest), Maharashtra, Karnataka. For sugar, jaggery, alcohol.
  • Tea: Assam, Darjeeling (WB), Nilgiris (TN). India = 2nd largest producer.
  • Coffee: Karnataka, Kerala, TN. Arabica and Robusta varieties.

🌱 Green Revolution (1960s–70s)

  • Led by M.S. Swaminathan. HYV seeds + fertilisers + irrigation = dramatic increase in food production.
  • Benefited mainly: Punjab, Haryana, Western UP. India became food self-sufficient.
  • Problems: Soil degradation, water table depletion, loss of crop diversity, environmental pollution.

💡 Key Facts

  • 52% of India's land is under cultivation. Agriculture employs ~60% of workforce. NABARD provides credit to farmers. India = world's largest producer of pulses, jute, milk, bananas, mangoes.

👥 Ch 3 — Human Resources

People are the most precious resource. This chapter covers population distribution, growth, composition, migration, and the Human Development Index.

🌏 Population Distribution

  • World population is unevenly distributed. 90% of people live in only 10% of land area.
  • Densely populated: East Asia, South Asia, Europe, Eastern North America.
  • Sparsely populated: Polar regions, Sahara Desert, Amazon forest, Himalayas.
  • Factors affecting distribution: Climate, water availability, fertile soil, flat terrain, resources, industrialisation.

📊 Key Demographic Terms

  • Population Density: Persons per km² = Population ÷ Area.
  • Birth Rate: Live births per 1000 persons per year.
  • Death Rate: Deaths per 1000 persons per year.
  • Natural Growth Rate: Birth Rate − Death Rate.
  • Life Expectancy: Average expected lifespan. High in developed nations (75-80 yrs).
  • Sex Ratio: Females per 1000 males. India 2011 = 943 females per 1000 males.
  • Literacy Rate: % of population aged 7+ who can read and write.

📂 Population Composition

  • Age groups: Young (0-14), Working age (15-64), Elderly (65+).
  • Population Pyramid: Visual of age-sex distribution. Wide base = young population (developing countries). Narrow base, wide middle = ageing population (developed countries).
  • Demographic Dividend: Economic benefit when most population is in working-age group — India has this advantage currently.

🔄 Migration

  • Push factors (why people LEAVE): Poverty, unemployment, drought, conflict, lack of services.
  • Pull factors (why people MOVE TO): Better jobs, wages, education, healthcare, safety.
  • Brain Drain: Emigration of highly skilled/educated people from developing to developed countries.

🎓 Human Development Index (HDI)

  • UNDP concept. Measures: Life expectancy + Education (literacy + schooling) + Per capita income (GNI).
  • Human Capital Formation: Investing in education, healthcare, and training to increase people's productivity.

💡 India Facts

  • India's 2011 Census = 1.21 billion. Most dense states: Bihar, West Bengal, UP, Kerala. Census every 10 years.

🌿 Ch 4 — Tribal Societies

Explores the rich tribal life before British rule, how colonialism destroyed it, and the powerful revolts that followed — especially Birsa Munda's Ulgulan.

🏕️ Traditional Tribal Life

  • Self-sufficient communities in forests, hills, and remote areas. Their own governance, customs, and languages.
  • Community ownership: Land and forests communally owned — no individual ownership concept.
  • Occupations: Shifting cultivation (Jhum), hunting, gathering forest produce, fishing, animal herding, handicrafts.
  • Religion: Nature worship — rivers, trees, hills, animals, spirits. Religion inseparable from the forest.

💔 How British Rule Destroyed Tribal Life

  • Forest Laws (Indian Forest Act 1878): Reserved, Protected, Village forests — denied tribals free access to forests they had lived in for centuries.
  • Loss of Livelihood: Without forests, tribals lost food, fuel, fodder, timber, medicines.
  • Dikus (outsiders): Moneylenders and traders who trapped tribals in debt, then seized their land. Tribals became landless labourers on their own ancestral land.
  • Forced Labour: Tea plantations (Assam), coal mines (Jharkhand), railway/road construction — harsh conditions, low wages.
  • Loss of Chiefs' Authority: British replaced tribal governance with their own system. Chiefs became powerless rent collectors.

⚔️ Tribal Revolts

  • Santhal Rebellion (1855–56): Led by brothers Sidhu and Kanhu. Rajmahal Hills region. Against British, zamindars, and moneylenders. Brutally suppressed.
  • Ulgulan / Munda Rebellion (1899–1900): Led by Birsa Munda. Ulgulan = "Great Tumult." Aimed to establish Munda Raj. Birsa revered as "Dharti Aba" (Father of the Earth). Arrested 1900, died in jail. His birthday (15 Nov) = Jharkhand formation day.
  • Khasi Uprising: Led by Tirot Singh in Meghalaya against British road construction.

🎨 Tribal Art Forms

  • Warli (Maharashtra) | Gond (MP) | Madhubani (Bihar) | Pattachitra (Odisha).

💡 Key Facts

  • Tribals = Adivasis (original inhabitants) = ~8.6% of India's population. Major tribes: Gond, Bhil, Santhal, Munda, Naga, Mizo. 5th Schedule of Constitution protects tribal areas.

⚔️ Ch 5 — Revolt of 1857

The first massive uprising against British rule — the First War of Independence — covering its causes, key leaders, why it failed, and its historic consequences.

📋 Five Root Causes

  • Political — Doctrine of Lapse: Lord Dalhousie annexed Satara (1848), Jhansi (1854), Awadh (1856) after rulers died without natural heirs.
  • Economic: British policies ruined Indian crafts/trade. Heavy land taxes. Wealth drained to Britain.
  • Social/Religious: Fear of forced Christianisation. Abolition of Sati (1829), Widow Remarriage Act (1856) seen as attacks on tradition.
  • Military: Indian soldiers got lower pay, could not be promoted beyond certain ranks, had to serve overseas (religiously polluting for Hindus).
  • Immediate — Greased Cartridges: New Enfield rifle cartridges rumoured to be greased with cow fat (Hindu taboo) and pig fat (Muslim taboo). Had to be bitten before loading. United both communities in outrage.

📅 Key Events and Leaders

  • March 1857: Mangal Pandey at Barrackpore refused cartridge, attacked British officers. Hanged 8 April 1857. First martyr.
  • 10 May 1857 — Meerut: Soldiers mutinied, marched to Delhi. The revolt officially begins.
  • Delhi — Bahadur Shah Zafar: Last Mughal emperor proclaimed leader. Gave the revolt a royal symbol.
  • Kanpur — Nana Saheb + Tantia Tope: Nana Saheb's pension had been stopped. Tantia Tope led guerrilla war until 1859.
  • Lucknow — Begum Hazrat Mahal: Led fierce resistance with her young son Birjis Qadr.
  • Jhansi — Rani Lakshmibai: Immortal symbol of revolt. Died in battle at Gwalior, June 1858. British General Hugh Rose called her "the best and bravest military leader of the rebels."
  • Bihar — Kunwar Singh: 70-year-old zamindar. Legendary guerrilla campaign. Cut off his own shot hand and died a hero.

🔴 Why the Revolt Failed

  • No unified leadership | Limited geographical spread (south India and Bengal unaffected) | Some Indian princes supported British | British had telegraph, superior weapons, and reinforcements | No single unifying ideology.

📝 Consequences

  • End of East India Company. India under British Crown directly (Queen Victoria's Proclamation, 1 November 1858). Lord Canning = first Viceroy. Bahadur Shah Zafar exiled to Rangoon. Sowed seeds of Indian nationalism.

📚 Ch 6 — Impact of British Rule on Education

British transformed India's education system to produce English-knowing clerks — but accidentally created a class that would use Western ideas to demand freedom from the British themselves.

📜 Traditional Education Before British

  • Pathshalas (vernacular village schools) | Madrasas (Islamic schools) | Gurukuls (Sanskrit/Vedic) | Tols (advanced Sanskrit). Community-funded, in local language, covered philosophy, astronomy, mathematics, medicine, grammar.

⚖️ Orientalists vs Anglicists

  • Orientalists: Supported traditional Indian learning in Sanskrit/Persian/Arabic. Argued it would preserve culture and gain Indian trust. Supported by Warren Hastings, William Jones.
  • Anglicists: Wanted English education to create cheap English-knowing clerks. Led by Charles Grant, James Mill.
  • Macaulay's Minute (1835): Decisively chose English. Said a "single shelf of a good European library was worth the entire native literature of India." English declared medium of higher education. Traditional funding stopped.

📋 Key Education Acts

  • Charter Act 1813: First British commitment — ₹1 lakh annually for Indian education.

⚡ Impact of English Education

  • Positive: New educated middle class. Common language for educated Indians across regions. Exposure to Western ideas of liberty, democracy, equality. Birth of Indian nationalism. Social reform movements.
  • Negative: Neglect of primary education. Traditional Indian learning marginalised. Education only for rich urban families — widening inequality. Created a class disconnected from Indian reality.

📜 Ch 8 — Our Constitution

The world's longest written constitution — its making, key features, Preamble, Fundamental Rights, Directive Principles, and the amendment process.

🏛️ Making the Constitution

  • Constituent Assembly (December 1946). Drafting Committee Chairman: Dr. B.R. Ambedkar — "Father of the Indian Constitution."
  • Took 2 years, 11 months, 18 days. Adopted: 26 November 1949 (Constitution Day). In effect: 26 January 1950 (Republic Day). World's longest written constitution.

📋 The Preamble — Key Lines

  • Begins: "We, the People of India..." — power comes from citizens.
  • Promises: Justice (social, economic, political) | Liberty (thought, expression, belief, faith, worship) | Equality (status, opportunity) | Fraternity (brotherhood, dignity, unity).

🌟 Five Key Features: SSDDR

  • Sovereign: No foreign control. India makes its own laws and foreign policy.
  • Socialist: Reduce inequalities. Resources not concentrated in few hands.
  • Secular: No state religion. All faiths equal before law.
  • Democratic: Government of, by, and for the people. Universal adult franchise (18+).
  • Republic: Elected head of state (President) — not hereditary. No monarchy.

📖 Three Main Parts

  • Fundamental Rights (Part III, Arts 12–35): Six rights. Enforceable by courts. Directly enforceable in the Supreme Court.
  • Directive Principles (Part IV, Arts 36–51): Guidelines to create a just welfare state. NOT enforceable in courts but fundamental to governance.
  • Fundamental Duties (Art 51A, 42nd Amendment 1976): 11 duties of citizens. Morally binding, not legally enforceable.

🏛️ Ch 9 — The Executive

India has a parliamentary system with a ceremonial President and a real Prime Minister. Understanding the difference between nominal and real executive power is key.

👑 President — Constitutional Head

  • Nominal/ceremonial head. All executive power exercised on advice of Council of Ministers.
  • Elected INDIRECTLY by Electoral College = elected MPs of both houses + elected MLAs of all states. Term: 5 years. Minimum age: 35.
  • Key Powers: Appoints PM, ministers, governors, SC/HC judges, CAG, CEC. Summons/dissolves Parliament. Assents to bills. Issues ordinances when Parliament not in session. Commander-in-Chief. Declares Emergency.
  • Removal by impeachment for violation of Constitution — 2/3 majority of both houses required.

🤝 Vice-President

  • Elected by members of both houses of Parliament. Ex-officio Chairman of Rajya Sabha. Acts as President during vacancy or incapacity.

👨‍💼 Prime Minister — Real Executive

  • Real head of government. Most powerful person. Appointed by President but must lead majority in Lok Sabha.
  • Heads Council of Ministers. Collective responsibility to Lok Sabha — if Cabinet loses majority, all must resign.
  • Three categories: Cabinet Ministers (senior, head key ministries) | Ministers of State (junior, independent or attached) | Deputy Ministers (assist senior ministers).

🏢 Governor — State Level

  • Constitutional head of a state. Appointed by President (NOT elected). Acts on advice of Chief Minister and state Council of Ministers.

💡 Key Comparisons

  • President ↔ Governor | PM ↔ Chief Minister | PM advises President ↔ CM advises Governor. President elected ↔ Governor appointed by President.

⚖️ Ch 10 — Judiciary

The independent Judiciary — guardian of the Constitution, protector of rights, and last resort for justice. Covers court structure, Supreme Court powers, the five writs, and PIL.

🏛️ Court Structure

  • Supreme Court (New Delhi) → High Courts (25, one per state/UT) → District Courts → Sub-Divisional/Magistrate Courts → Lok Adalats (people's courts for speedy settlement).

🔑 Five Powers of the Supreme Court

  • Original Jurisdiction (Art 131): Exclusive power to hear disputes between states or between states and Centre.
  • Appellate Jurisdiction: Appeals against High Court decisions in constitutional, civil, and criminal matters.
  • Advisory Jurisdiction (Art 143): President can seek SC's legal opinion (not binding).
  • Writ Jurisdiction (Art 32): Citizens can petition SC directly for enforcement of Fundamental Rights. (Dr. Ambedkar: "Heart and Soul of the Constitution.")
  • Judicial Review: Power to declare any law or executive action unconstitutional and void. Makes SC the ultimate guardian of the Constitution.

📋 Five Writs — Complete Reference

  • 1. Habeas Corpus ("You shall have the body"): Orders detaining authority to produce detained person in court and justify the detention. Protects against illegal imprisonment.
  • 2. Mandamus ("We command"): Orders a public official to perform a duty they are legally required to do but have refused. Cannot be issued against President, Governor, or private persons.
  • 3. Prohibition: Issued by higher court to STOP a lower court/tribunal from proceeding beyond its jurisdiction. Preventive — issued before wrong is done.
  • 4. Certiorari ("To be certified"): Issued to QUASH (cancel) order already passed by a lower court/tribunal if it exceeded jurisdiction. Corrective — issued after wrong is done.
  • 5. Quo Warranto ("By what authority"): Requires a person to prove by what authority they hold a public office. Prevents illegal usurpation of public positions.

🎓 Judicial Independence

  • Appointed through collegium system — not by government alone. SC judges retire at 65, HC judges at 62. Removal only by impeachment (very complex). Salaries charged to Consolidated Fund (not subject to parliamentary vote). All these ensure fearless, impartial justice.

🌟 PIL (Public Interest Litigation)

  • Any citizen can petition SC/HC to protect public interest — even if not personally affected. Introduced 1980s by Justice P.N. Bhagwati. Used to fight pollution, protect prisoners' rights, ensure right to food, address corruption.

🕊️ Ch 11 — Fundamental Rights

The six fundamental rights guaranteed to every Indian citizen by the Constitution — their full detail, scope, restrictions, and the critical distinction from Directive Principles.

📋 The Six Fundamental Rights

  • 1. Right to Equality (Arts 14–18): Art 14: Equality before law + equal protection. Art 15: No discrimination by state on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth. Art 16: Equal opportunity in public employment. Art 17: Abolition of untouchability — practising it is a criminal offence. Art 18: Abolition of titles (no "Sir," "Lord" etc. from state).
  • 2. Right to Freedom (Arts 19–22): Art 19: Six freedoms — speech, peaceful assembly, association, movement throughout India, residence anywhere, profession/trade. Art 20: Three protections — no ex post facto law, no double jeopardy, no self-incrimination. Art 21: Right to LIFE and personal liberty. SC has expanded to include privacy, livelihood, health, speedy trial, clean environment. Art 21A (86th Amendment 2002): Free and compulsory education for children aged 6–14. Art 22: Protection against arbitrary arrest — must be told grounds, can consult a lawyer, must be produced before magistrate within 24 hours.
  • 3. Right Against Exploitation (Arts 23–24): Art 23: Prohibits human trafficking, begar (forced unpaid labour). Art 24: No child below 14 years in factory, mine, or hazardous employment.
  • 4. Right to Freedom of Religion (Arts 25–28): Art 25: Freedom to profess, practise, propagate any religion. Art 26: Religious denominations can manage their own affairs. Art 27: No compulsory taxes for any particular religion. Art 28: No religious instruction in state-funded institutions.
  • 5. Cultural & Educational Rights (Arts 29–30): Art 29: Right to conserve one's distinct language, script, culture. Art 30: Minorities can establish and administer educational institutions.
  • 6. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Art 32): Called "Heart and Soul of the Constitution" by Dr. Ambedkar. Citizens can go DIRECTLY to the Supreme Court to enforce fundamental rights through the five writs.

⚠️ Restrictions and Suspension

  • Not absolute — subject to reasonable restrictions for state security, public order, morality.
  • Can be suspended during National Emergency EXCEPT Arts 20 and 21 — which can NEVER be suspended.

📝 Right to Property

  • Originally a Fundamental Right but removed by the 44th Amendment (1978). Now only a legal right (Art 300A). State can acquire property only by following law and paying compensation.

💡 Key Distinction

  • Fundamental Rights = enforceable by courts, protect individuals from state action.
  • Directive Principles = not enforceable, guide government policy for social welfare.
  • Fundamental Duties = morally binding on citizens, not legally enforceable.

🔢 Ch 1 — Squares & Square Roots

📋 Perfect Squares

  • n² = n×n. List 1²–15²: 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144, 169, 196, 225.
  • Only end in 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, 9. NEVER 2, 3, 7, or 8. Square of even = even. Square of odd = odd.
  • Difference of consecutive squares: n² − (n−1)² = 2n−1 (consecutive odd numbers).

🔍 Square Root Methods

  • Prime Factorisation: Factorise → group into pairs → take one from each pair → multiply. √324 = √(2×2×3×3×3×3) = 2×3×3 = 18.
  • Long Division: Works for ALL numbers including non-perfect squares. Group digits in pairs from right.
  • Repeated Subtraction: Subtract consecutive odd numbers (1,3,5…) until zero. Count = square root. Only for perfect squares.

📐 Pythagorean Triplets

  • (3,4,5), (5,12,13), (6,8,10), (7,24,25), (8,15,17), (9,40,41). General: (2m, m²−1, m²+1) for m>1.

🧮 Properties

  • √(a×b) = √a × √b. ⚠️ √(a+b) ≠ √a + √b.
  • To make a perfect square: prime factorise → find unpaired factors → those are what you multiply/divide by.

🎲 Ch 2 — Cubes & Cube Roots

📦 Perfect Cubes

  • 1³=1, 2³=8, 3³=27, 4³=64, 5³=125, 6³=216, 7³=343, 8³=512, 9³=729, 10³=1000.
  • Even cube = even. Odd cube = odd. Negative cube = negative.
  • Unit digit pattern: 1→1, 2→8, 3→7, 4→4, 5→5, 6→6, 7→3, 8→2, 9→9, 0→0.

✅ Checking and Finding Cube Root

  • Prime factorise → form triplets. All factors in complete triplets = perfect cube.
  • ∛1728 = ∛(2³×2³×3³) = 2×2×3 = 12.
  • To make perfect cube: find incomplete triplets → multiply/divide by what's needed to complete them.

🧮 Properties

  • ∛(a×b) = ∛a × ∛b. ∛(a³) = a.

⚡ Ch 3 — Exponents & Radicals

📋 Seven Laws of Exponents

  • 1. aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ (same base: ADD powers)
  • 2. aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ (same base: SUBTRACT powers)
  • 3. (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ (power of power: MULTIPLY)
  • 4. (ab)ᵐ = aᵐ × bᵐ (distribute to each factor)
  • 5. (a/b)ᵐ = aᵐ/bᵐ (distribute to numerator and denominator)
  • 6. a⁰ = 1 (any non-zero base to power 0)
  • 7. a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ (negative exponent = reciprocal)

🌐 Standard Form

  • Format: m × 10ⁿ where 1 ≤ m < 10. Large number (5,700,000) = 5.7 × 10⁶. Small number (0.00034) = 3.4 × 10⁻⁴.
  • Laws: 1ⁿ = 1 always. (-1)ⁿ = +1 if n even, −1 if n odd. 0ⁿ = 0 for n>0.

🔄 Ch 4 — Direct & Inverse Variation

📈 Direct Variation

  • x/y = k (constant ratio). x₁/y₁ = x₂/y₂. Both increase/decrease together.
  • Examples: Cost & quantity, distance & time at fixed speed, workers & total wages.

📉 Inverse Variation

  • x × y = k (constant product). x₁y₁ = x₂y₂. One increases as the other decreases.
  • Examples: Speed & time (fixed distance), workers & days (fixed job), pipes & time to fill tank.

🧠 Quick Rule

  • "Both go same direction → Direct. Opposite directions → Inverse."

💰 Ch 5 — Profit, Loss & Discount

💡 Definitions

  • CP = buying price. SP = selling price. MP = marked price on label. Profit when SP>CP. Loss when SP<CP.

🧮 All Formulas

  • Profit = SP−CP. Loss = CP−SP. Profit% = (Profit/CP)×100. Loss% = (Loss/CP)×100.
  • SP = CP×(100+P%)/100 or CP×(100−L%)/100.
  • CP = SP×100/(100+P%) or SP×100/(100−L%).
  • Discount = MP−SP. Discount% = (Discount/MP)×100. SP = MP×(100−D%)/100.

⚡ Successive Discounts

  • d₁% then d₂%: Effective = d₁+d₂−(d₁×d₂)/100. Example: 10%+20% = 28% (NOT 30%).

⚠️ Golden Rule

  • Profit%/Loss% → ALWAYS on CP. Discount% → ALWAYS on MP.

📈 Ch 6 — Compound Interest

📋 SI vs CI

  • SI: Only on original principal. Linear growth. SI = PRT/100.
  • CI: On principal + accumulated interest. Exponential growth. CI always > SI for T>1 year.

🧮 All CI Formulas

  • Annually: A = P(1+R/100)ⁿ. CI = A−P.
  • Half-yearly: A = P(1+R/200)²ⁿ.
  • Quarterly: A = P(1+R/400)⁴ⁿ.
  • CI−SI (2 years) = P(R/100)². CI−SI (3 years) = P(R/100)²(3+R/100).
  • Population after n years = P₀(1+r/100)ⁿ. Depreciation = Original×(1−r/100)ⁿ.

📅 Year-by-Year Method

  • Year 1: Interest = P×R/100. New principal = P+Interest. Year 2: Interest = New principal×R/100. Continue. Use to VERIFY formula answers.

🔣 Ch 7 — Algebraic Identities

📋 All Nine Standard Identities

  • 1: (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²
  • 2: (a−b)² = a²−2ab+b²
  • 3: (a+b)(a−b) = a²−b²
  • 4: (x+a)(x+b) = x²+(a+b)x+ab
  • 5: (a+b+c)² = a²+b²+c²+2ab+2bc+2ca
  • 6: (a+b)³ = a³+3a²b+3ab²+b³
  • 7: (a−b)³ = a³−3a²b+3ab²−b³
  • 8: a³+b³ = (a+b)(a²−ab+b²)
  • 9: a³−b³ = (a−b)(a²+ab+b²)

🔑 Derived Formulas

📝 Quick Calculations using Identities

📊 Ch 8 — Polynomials

📋 Classification by Terms

  • Monomial (1 term): 3x². Binomial (2 terms): x+5. Trinomial (3 terms): x²+2x+1. Polynomial (4+ terms).

📋 Classification by Degree

  • Degree 0 = Constant (7). Degree 1 = Linear (ax+b). Degree 2 = Quadratic (ax²+bx+c). Degree 3 = Cubic. Zero polynomial: degree undefined.

🔑 Key Terms

  • Coefficient = numerical multiplier. Leading term = highest degree term. Constant term = term with no variable = p(0). Like terms = same variable, same power.

🧮 Operations

  • Add/Subtract: Combine LIKE TERMS only. Multiply: Each term × each term, then collect like terms. Degree of product = sum of individual degrees.

🔗 Ch 9 — Linear Equations

📋 Basics

  • Form: ax+b=0 (a≠0). Degree 1. Exactly ONE solution. Represents a straight line on graph.

🔑 Solving Rules

  • Add/subtract/multiply/divide the SAME thing to BOTH sides. Transposition: Moving a term to the other side changes its sign.

📝 Steps

  • 1. Expand brackets. 2. Collect variable terms on LEFT. 3. Collect constants on RIGHT. 4. Divide by coefficient of variable. 5. VERIFY by substituting back — LHS must equal RHS.

🔄 Cross Multiplication

  • a/b = c/d → ad = bc. (px+q)/(rx+s) = a/b → b(px+q) = a(rx+s). Expand, collect, solve.

💡 Word Problems Strategy

  • Let unknown = x → express all quantities in terms of x → form equation → solve → verify it satisfies original conditions.

📐 Ch 10 — Parallel Lines

📐 Angle Relationships (lines ARE parallel)

  • ✅ Corresponding angles = EQUAL (F-shape)
  • ✅ Alternate interior angles = EQUAL (Z-shape)
  • ✅ Alternate exterior angles = EQUAL
  • ➕ Co-interior (allied) angles = SUPPLEMENTARY = 180° (C-shape)

📐 Always True (even NOT parallel)

  • ✅ Vertically opposite angles = EQUAL. ➕ Linear pair = 180°. ➕ Angles at a point = 360°.

🔑 Converse — Proving Lines Parallel

  • Corresponding angles equal → lines parallel. Alternate interior angles equal → lines parallel. Co-interior angles = 180° → lines parallel.

💡 Triangle Properties

  • Sum of angles in triangle = 180°. Exterior angle = sum of two non-adjacent interior angles. Sum of angles in quadrilateral = 360°.

🔷 Ch 11 — Quadrilaterals

📋 General Rule

  • 4 sides, 4 angles, 4 vertices. Sum of all interior angles = 360°. 2 diagonals.

🔷 Properties — All Types

  • Parallelogram: Opp sides equal & parallel. Opp angles equal. Consecutive angles supplementary. Diagonals BISECT each other (not equal, not ⊥).
  • Rectangle: Parallelogram properties + all angles = 90° + diagonals EQUAL and bisect each other.
  • Rhombus: Parallelogram properties + all 4 sides equal + diagonals bisect at RIGHT ANGLES (⊥) but NOT equal.
  • Square: Rectangle + Rhombus properties combined. All sides equal + all angles 90° + diagonals equal, ⊥, bisect each other and vertex angles. PERFECT quadrilateral.
  • Trapezium: Exactly ONE pair of parallel sides. Area = ½(a+b)h. Isosceles trapezium has equal legs and equal diagonals.
  • Kite: Two pairs of ADJACENT sides equal. One diagonal ⊥ bisects the other. One pair of opposite angles equal.

🔑 Hierarchy

  • Square ⊂ Rectangle ⊂ Parallelogram. Square ⊂ Rhombus ⊂ Parallelogram. Every square is also a rectangle, rhombus, and parallelogram.

📊 Ch 14 — Statistics & Probability

📋 Measures of Central Tendency

  • Mean: Sum ÷ Count. Affected by extreme values. Best for uniform data.
  • Median: Middle value (data sorted). n odd: ((n+1)/2)th value. n even: average of (n/2)th and (n/2+1)th. NOT affected by extremes.
  • Mode: Most frequent value. Can have 0, 1, or multiple modes. Best for categorical data.
  • Range: Max − Min. Measures spread.

📊 Frequency Distribution

  • Frequency = number of times a value occurs. Tally marks for counting (IIII with diagonal = 5). Class interval = range of values grouped together. Cumulative frequency = running total.

🎲 Probability

  • P(E) = Favourable outcomes / Total outcomes. 0 ≤ P(E) ≤ 1. P(certain) = 1. P(impossible) = 0. P(E) + P(E') = 1.

💪 Ch 1 — Force & Pressure

Force and pressure explain motion, stillness, and interaction. From walking to submarines to hydraulic brakes — all explained by these two concepts.

🔑 Force

  • Force = push or pull. SI unit = Newton (N). Vector quantity (has direction).
  • Five Effects: Makes stationary object move | Stops a moving object | Changes speed | Changes direction | Changes shape or size.
  • Contact Forces (require physical contact): Muscular force (muscles), Friction (opposing motion), Normal force (perpendicular to surface), Tension (in stretched string), Spring force.
  • Non-Contact Forces (act at a distance): Gravitational (attraction between masses, always pulls toward Earth's centre), Magnetic (can attract or repel), Electrostatic (between charged objects — like charges repel, unlike attract).

⚖️ Balanced vs Unbalanced Forces

  • Balanced: Forces cancel out (equal magnitude, opposite direction). Object stays still or moves at constant velocity. No acceleration.
  • Unbalanced: Net force ≠ 0. Object accelerates, decelerates, or changes direction. Newton's Second Law: F = ma.

📏 Pressure = Force ÷ Area

  • SI unit = Pascal (Pa) = N/m². Same force on smaller area = MORE pressure. On larger area = LESS pressure.
  • Increase pressure: Sharpen edge — knife, needle, nails, pointed heels, drawing pins, cutting tools.
  • Decrease pressure: Widen the base — snowshoes, camel's padded feet, wide truck tyres, broad building foundations, backpack with wide straps.

💧 Liquid Pressure

  • Acts in ALL directions at any point. Increases with DEPTH (dams thicker at base). Depends on density of liquid.
  • Pascal's Law: Pressure applied to confined liquid transmitted equally in all directions. Used in hydraulic brakes, jacks, presses, and lifts.

🌬️ Atmospheric Pressure

  • Air pressure at sea level ≈ 101,325 Pa. Decreases with altitude. Measured by barometer (invented by Torricelli).
  • Real-Life Applications: Drinking straw — reduced air pressure inside → atmospheric pressure pushes liquid up. Suction cups — low pressure area → atmospheric pressure holds them. Syringes. Rubber suckers. Siphons.

🔵 Ch 2 — Friction

Friction opposes relative motion. It is both a friend (lets us walk, drive, hold objects) and an enemy (wastes energy, causes wear). Understanding it helps design better machines and surfaces.

🔑 What is Friction?

  • A contact force opposing relative motion or tendency of motion between surfaces in contact.
  • Caused by interlocking of microscopic irregularities (bumps and grooves) on all surfaces.

📂 Four Types (in INCREASING order)

  • Rolling Friction: Object rolls on surface. LEAST friction. That's why wheels were invented.
  • Kinetic/Sliding Friction: Object slides on surface. More than rolling. That's why sliding is harder than rolling.
  • Static Friction: Object at rest with force applied but not yet moving. HIGHEST of the three. That's why it's hardest to START pushing a heavy object.
  • Fluid Friction (Drag): Object moving through liquid or gas. Also called air resistance or drag.
  • Order: Rolling < Kinetic/Sliding < Static

📊 What Friction Depends On

  • Nature of surfaces (rougher = more friction). Normal force (heavier = more friction).
  • Friction does NOT depend on: speed of object, area of contact, or shape (for same material and normal force).

✅ Advantages — We NEED Friction

  • Walking (shoe sole grips ground). Writing (pen/pencil on paper). Braking (brake pads on disc). Holding objects (grip). Striking matches (friction creates heat → ignition). Belt drives in machines. Climbing ropes and ladders.

❌ Disadvantages — We Want to REDUCE It

  • Wastes energy as heat. Causes wear and tear. Reduces machine efficiency. Generates heat (needs cooling in engines).

🔧 Methods to REDUCE Friction

  • Lubricants: Oil, grease, graphite — thin film prevents direct contact. Used in engines, bicycle chains, hinges.
  • Ball Bearings: Converts sliding to rolling friction (much smaller). Used in cycles, motors, fans, car wheel hubs.
  • Streamlining: Smooth aerodynamic shape reduces fluid friction (drag). Aircraft, fast trains, ships, fish, dolphins, bullets.
  • Polished surfaces. Air-cushion vehicles (hovercraft). Powder between moving parts.

🔧 Methods to INCREASE Friction

  • Grooves/treads on tyres (wet road grip). Rough shoe soles. Chalk on gymnasts' hands. Sand on icy roads. Serrated edges on files and sandpaper. Brake pads made of high-friction material.

🔊 Ch 3 — Sound

Sound is mechanical energy that travels as a longitudinal wave. This chapter covers how sound is produced and travels, its characteristics, hearing range, and real-world applications from SONAR to medical ultrasound.

🔑 What is Sound?

  • Produced by vibrating objects. When object vibrates → pushes and pulls surrounding medium particles → creates alternating compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure) → wave travels outward.
  • Sound is a mechanical longitudinal wave — particles oscillate in the SAME direction as wave travel. Needs a material medium (solid, liquid, gas). CANNOT travel through vacuum.

🌊 Speed of Sound

  • Speed: Solid > Liquid > Gas. Particles in solids are closest together → vibrations transfer fastest.
  • Speed in air at 25°C ≈ 343 m/s. In water ≈ 1480 m/s. In steel ≈ 5120 m/s.
  • Speed increases with temperature (warmer air molecules move faster, carry vibrations faster).

📊 Four Properties of Sound

  • Frequency (f): Vibrations per second. Unit = Hertz (Hz). Determines PITCH. High frequency = high pitch (shrill). Low frequency = low pitch (deep bass).
  • Amplitude (A): Maximum displacement of vibrating particles. Determines LOUDNESS. High amplitude = loud. Measured in decibels (dB).
  • Wavelength (λ): Distance between two consecutive compressions (or rarefactions).
  • Time Period (T): Time for one complete vibration. T = 1/f. Speed = Frequency × Wavelength (v = fλ).

🎵 Hearing Ranges

  • Audible range (humans): 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz (20 kHz). We can only hear within this range.
  • Infrasonic (<20 Hz): Below human range. Produced by earthquakes, volcanoes, large animals. Elephants and whales communicate using infrasound over hundreds of kilometres.
  • Ultrasonic (>20,000 Hz): Above human range. Bats: up to 100,000 Hz. Dolphins: up to 150,000 Hz. Dogs: up to 65,000 Hz. Used by these animals for echolocation (navigation and hunting).

🔄 Real-Life Applications

  • Echo: Reflection of sound from a hard surface. Minimum distance for echo = 17.2 m (so returning sound arrives at least 1/10 sec after original ends). Used by bats, dolphins for navigation.
  • SONAR (Sound Navigation And Ranging): Emits ultrasonic pulses underwater. Measures time for echo to return → calculates distance to object (submarines, seabed, fish schools, shipwrecks).
  • Medical Ultrasound (Sonography): High-frequency sound waves image internal organs without surgery. Checks foetal development, detects kidney stones, tumours, blockages.
  • Ultrasound Cleaning: Ultrasonic vibrations dislodge dirt particles from delicate items — jewellery, dental instruments, machine parts, electronic components.
  • Hearing Aid: Amplifies sound waves for people with hearing loss.

🔊 Noise Pollution

  • Excessive/unpleasant sound harmful to health. Sources: traffic, loudspeakers, industrial machines, firecrackers, aircraft.
  • Effects: Hearing impairment (above 85 dB for long periods), stress, hypertension, sleep problems, reduced concentration.
  • Control: Sound-absorbing materials, noise barriers, regulations on loudspeakers, planting trees, catalytic converters in vehicles.

⚡ Ch 4 — Chemical Effects of Electric Current

Electric current causes chemical reactions in conducting solutions — the basis of electroplating, electrolysis, and key industrial processes. Understanding conductors, electrolytes, and electrodes is essential.

🔑 Conductors and Insulators

  • Conductors: Allow electric current to flow. Metals (copper, silver, iron, aluminium), graphite, human body, acids, salt solutions.
  • Insulators: Do not allow current. Rubber, plastic, wood, glass, pure/distilled water, dry air, ceramics.
  • Simple Tester: Battery + bulb/LED + connecting wires. If bulb lights up when material is connected → conductor. LED glows for even weak current (better for detecting weak conductors like lemon juice).

💧 Electrolytes

  • Electrolytes (conduct electricity): Substances that dissociate into ions in water. Common salt (NaCl), copper sulphate (CuSO₄), dilute sulphuric acid, lemon juice, vinegar, salt water.
  • Non-electrolytes (do NOT conduct): Sugar solution, distilled water, alcohol, glucose. No ions form, so no current flows.
  • Lemon juice and vinegar are WEAK acids — they conduct enough to light an LED but not a bulb.

⚗️ Electrolysis

  • Chemical decomposition of a substance using electric current when in dissolved or molten state.
  • Anode (+): Connected to positive terminal. Negative ions (anions) move here. Oxidation occurs.
  • Cathode (−): Connected to negative terminal. Positive ions (cations) move here and are deposited. Reduction occurs.
  • Electrolysis of water: Hydrogen gas at cathode (2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂). Oxygen gas at anode. Ratio H₂:O₂ = 2:1 by volume.

🔶 Electroplating — Full Detail

  • Coating one metal with a thin layer of another using electrolysis.
  • Setup: Object to be plated = CATHODE (−). Plating metal = ANODE (+). Salt solution of plating metal = ELECTROLYTE.
  • Example — Gold plating cheap jewellery: Cheap metal object = cathode. Gold sheet = anode. Gold salt solution = electrolyte. Current flows → gold ions dissolve from anode → deposit on cathode (the jewellery). Result: Thin gold layer on cheap metal.
  • Example — Galvanisation (zinc coating on iron): Iron object = cathode. Zinc sheet = anode. Zinc sulphate solution = electrolyte. Prevents iron from rusting.

💡 Uses of Electroplating

  • Prevent corrosion: Galvanised iron (zinc), tin-plated food cans, chrome-plated car parts.
  • Improve appearance: Gold/silver plating on jewellery, medals, trophies, decorative items.
  • Reduce friction: Hard chrome on engine cylinders and pistons.
  • Medical: Silver/gold coating for biocompatibility (implants, surgical instruments).
  • Electronics: Copper coating on circuit boards, gold plating on electrical contacts for reliability.

🌟 Other Effects of Electric Current (for comparison)

  • Chemical effect: Electrolysis, electroplating. Heating effect: Electric heater, toaster, electric bulb (tungsten filament). Magnetic effect: Electromagnets, electric motors (Oersted discovered this). Luminous effect: LEDs, fluorescent tubes.

💡 Ch 5 — Light

Light is electromagnetic energy that enables vision. This chapter covers reflection (plane and curved mirrors), refraction, lenses, dispersion, and real-world applications.

🔑 Nature of Light

  • Electromagnetic wave — travels through vacuum. Speed in vacuum = 3 × 10⁸ m/s (fastest in the universe).
  • Travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation) — proven by formation of sharp shadows and eclipses.
  • Transverse wave — oscillations perpendicular to direction of travel.

🔆 Laws of Reflection

  • Law 1: Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection (∠i = ∠r). Both measured from the NORMAL (perpendicular to surface).
  • Law 2: Incident ray, normal, and reflected ray are all in the same plane.
  • Plane Mirror Image: Virtual (behind mirror, cannot project on screen) | Erect (upright) | Same size as object | Same distance behind mirror as object is in front | Laterally inverted (left↔right — that's why AMBULANCE is written in reverse).

🔮 Curved Mirrors

  • Concave Mirror (converging): Curves INWARD (like inside of spoon). Converges parallel rays to a focal point in front. Uses: Shaving/makeup mirror (magnified image when close), dentist's mirror, torch/searchlight/car headlight reflectors, solar furnaces.
  • Convex Mirror (diverging): Curves OUTWARD (like outside of spoon). Diverges parallel rays. Image always virtual, erect, and diminished (smaller). Uses: Rear-view mirrors (wider field of view), security/surveillance mirrors in shops, road safety mirrors at blind corners, ATM mirrors.
  • Memory: Concave = cave-shaped = sunken inward. Convex = bulging outward.

🌊 Refraction

  • Bending of light as it passes from one medium to another due to change in SPEED.
  • Air → glass (denser): Speed decreases. Bends TOWARD the normal. Angle of refraction < angle of incidence.
  • Glass → air (less dense): Speed increases. Bends AWAY from normal. Angle of refraction > angle of incidence.
  • At 90° (normal incidence): No bending — passes straight through.
  • Applications: Apparent depth (pool looks shallower than it is). Twinkling of stars (light refracted by different layers of atmosphere). Mirages (desert — hot air near ground refracts light upward, looks like water).

🔍 Lenses

  • Convex Lens (converging): Thicker at centre. Converges parallel rays. Magnifying glass. Eye lens. Camera lens. Projector. Spectacles for hypermetropia (long-sightedness — can't see nearby objects).
  • Concave Lens (diverging): Thinner at centre. Diverges parallel rays. Image always virtual, erect, diminished. Spectacles for myopia (short-sightedness — can't see distant objects).

🌈 Dispersion of Light

  • Splitting of white light into its component colours (spectrum) by a prism. Each colour has different wavelength → bends by different amount in glass.
  • VIBGYOR: Violet (most bent, highest frequency, shortest wavelength) → Indigo → Blue → Green → Yellow → Orange → Red (least bent, lowest frequency, longest wavelength).
  • Rainbow: Sunlight dispersed by millions of water droplets in atmosphere after rain. Back to sun → rainbow visible as arc. Primary rainbow: red on outside, violet on inside.

👁️ Human Eye and Defects

  • Light enters → Cornea → Pupil (controlled by iris) → Lens (flexible, adjustable) → Retina → Optic nerve → Brain.
  • Myopia (near-sightedness): Can see nearby objects clearly, distant objects blurred. Image forms in FRONT of retina. Eyeball too long or lens too thick. Corrected by CONCAVE (diverging) lens.
  • Hypermetropia (long-sightedness / far-sightedness): Can see distant objects clearly, nearby objects blurred. Image forms BEHIND retina. Eyeball too short or lens too thin. Corrected by CONVEX (converging) lens.
